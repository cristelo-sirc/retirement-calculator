RETIREMENT CALCULATOR AUDIT: FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Date: June 27, 2026

PURPOSE
=======

This report summarizes a read-only review of the current application, with special
attention to:

1. Issues that can meaningfully affect projection accuracy.
2. Latency when changing inputs in Rework or applying suggested adjustments.
3. Reliability, accessibility, maintainability, and production readiness.

No existing application files were changed as part of the audit.


EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
=================

The application is a strong and unusually polished prototype. Its editorial design,
plain-language guidance, deterministic simulations, and Rework draft/publish model are
all good product decisions.

The Monte Carlo engine does not appear fundamentally broken. For a typical younger
household using ordinary assumptions, its results are likely directionally useful.
However, precise results should not yet be treated as production-grade financial
projections. Several issues can materially affect particular users, especially older
households, high-income Medicare couples, maximum retirement-plan savers, and plans
whose success score is close to a decision threshold.

The latency problem has a clear cause: every small input change performs report-grade
work on the browser's main thread. At the default 5,000-path setting, one full compute
performs approximately 15,165 lifetime simulations and builds large chart datasets,
even when Rework needs only a success score and verdict.


PART 1: PROJECTION-ACCURACY FINDINGS
====================================

1. RMD START AGE IS ALWAYS 75
-----------------------------

Severity: High for older users; low for the default 55-year-old example.

The engine begins required minimum distributions at age 75 for everyone. Current law
is cohort-dependent: age 73 generally applies to people who reach 73 before 2033, while
age 75 applies to later cohorts. The UI permits older users, so this is not merely a
theoretical edge case.

Possible effect:

- Pre-tax balances may remain invested too long.
- Taxable income and Medicare surcharges may be understated or shifted to later years.
- Roth-conversion opportunities may be overstated.
- Account balances and success rates may be distorted for affected cohorts.

Recommendation:

- Add a projection start year or derive it from the current calendar year.
- Infer birth year from current age, preferably with an explicit birth-year input.
- Determine the applicable RMD age by cohort.
- Add tests for users reaching RMD age at 73 and at 75.

Official reference:
https://www.irs.gov/irb/2026-06_IRB


2. SOCIAL SECURITY FULL RETIREMENT AGE IS ALWAYS 67
----------------------------------------------------

Severity: High for older cohorts; low for people born in 1960 or later.

The engine uses age 67 as full retirement age for every user and spouse. Actual full
retirement age varies by birth year for people born before 1960.

Possible effect:

- Early-claiming reductions can be wrong.
- Delayed-retirement credits can be wrong.
- Spousal-benefit comparisons can be wrong.
- Suggested claiming changes can show inaccurate impacts.

Recommendation:

- Calculate full retirement age separately for each partner from birth year.
- Use month-level ages if Social Security precision is an important product goal.
- Add golden tests for birth years 1954, 1957, 1959, and 1960.

Official reference:
https://www.ssa.gov/oact/progdata/nra.html


3. COUPLE IRMAA SURCHARGES APPEAR TO BE CHARGED ONLY ONCE
---------------------------------------------------------

Severity: Potentially high for higher-income Medicare couples.

The simulation counts how many partners are on Medicare, but the resulting IRMAA
surcharge is added only once rather than multiplied or calculated per covered person.
The calculation also uses current-year income rather than the normal two-year MAGI
lookback.

Possible effect:

- Retirement expenses and portfolio withdrawals may be understated.
- Roth conversions can look more attractive than they really are.
- Success rates can be overstated for households near IRMAA thresholds.

Recommendation:

- Calculate IRMAA separately for each Medicare-covered partner.
- Model the two-year income lookback, including a documented initialization rule for
  the first two simulated years.
- Version thresholds and surcharge amounts by calendar year.
- Test threshold crossings caused by RMDs and Roth conversions.


4. TAX AND CONTRIBUTION ASSUMPTIONS ARE STILL BASED ON 2025
-----------------------------------------------------------

Severity: Medium generally; higher for maximum savers and tax-sensitive strategies.

The app is labeled May 2026, but the simulation starts in 2025 and uses 2025 base tax
brackets, Social Security limits, IRMAA values, and retirement-plan contribution limits.
Some bracket values are grown by an inflation assumption, which gets reasonably close
to 2026 values, but it is not the same as using enacted annual values.

The 401(k) limit is particularly important because it remains fixed throughout the
projection. High savers can therefore have future contributions and balances
understated.

The optional higher-future-tax-rates setting still uses pre-TCJA brackets, although the
lower TCJA rates were made permanent in 2025. It may remain useful as a stress scenario,
but it should not be presented as a scheduled-law sunset.

Recommendation:

- Create a versioned assumptions table keyed by calendar year.
- Use enacted values for known years and a clearly documented growth rule afterward.
- Update the current-year 401(k), catch-up, Social Security earnings-test, tax, and
  IRMAA values.
- Rename the tax toggle as an explicit stress test, such as "Model higher future tax
  rates," and document exactly which schedule it applies.
- Display an "Assumptions updated through" date in the product.

Official references:
https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-releases-tax-inflation-adjustments-for-tax-year-2026-including-amendments-from-the-one-big-beautiful-bill
https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/401k-limit-increases-to-24500-for-2026-ira-limit-increases-to-7500
https://www.ssa.gov/cola/factsheets/2026.html


5. INPUTS ARE NOT FULLY VALIDATED OR NORMALIZED
------------------------------------------------

Severity: High when malformed or contradictory data is present.

The visible controls have minimums and maximums, but related values are not normalized
together. Loaded JSON and localStorage data are merged into defaults without complete
type, range, or relationship validation.

Examples:

- Current age can become equal to or greater than retirement age.
- Retirement age can exceed the plan end age.
- A partner's retirement age can conflict with the partner's current age.
- String values and string booleans can be interpreted incorrectly.
- A malformed or extremely large numPaths value can freeze the browser.
- Suggested moves can produce values outside the sliders' supported ranges.

Recommendation:

- Define one schema for all parameters, including type, minimum, maximum, enum values,
  and cross-field rules.
- Validate defaults, localStorage, imported files, and every committed update through
  the same schema.
- Reject invalid files with specific messages rather than silently coercing values.
- Clamp or update dependent ages together.
- Enforce a hard numPaths ceiling inside the engine, not only in the UI.


6. SOCIAL SECURITY SUGGESTIONS ARE INCONSISTENT ACROSS SCREENS
--------------------------------------------------------------

Severity: Medium for projection decisions; does not corrupt the filed base plan.

The Cover and Income & Odds calculations change both partners to age 70. Rework changes
only the primary user's claiming age.

Using the default plan:

- Filed plan: 42/100
- Rework's user-only change: 44/100
- Both partners changed, as used elsewhere: 46/100

The spending suggestion also shows +21 points on the Cover but +20 using the full path
count. Suggested moves are hard-coded in Rework and can be offered even when a change,
especially delaying Social Security over a short horizon, reduces success.

Recommendation:

- Define each scenario once as a reusable parameter patch.
- Use that same scenario definition and cached result everywhere.
- State clearly whether a claiming recommendation applies to one or both partners.
- Rank and display only moves that actually improve the current plan.
- Label low-sample deltas as estimates, or calculate all displayed deltas with the same
  authoritative path count.


7. PAYCHECK AND INCOME-SOURCE DISPLAYS DO NOT RECONCILE
-------------------------------------------------------

Severity: High for user interpretation; the core success score still includes spouse
salary internally.

The paycheck display uses the primary user's first retirement year. If a younger spouse
is still working, the spouse's salary is omitted from the displayed source breakdown.
The chart receives actual portfolio withdrawals but visually fills the entire gap
between guaranteed income and spending as portfolio funding.

In the default plan, the app states a need of about $12,267 per month but displays only
about $4,931 per month of sources, or 40% of the need.

Recommendation:

- Decide whether the featured year is the primary user's retirement or the household's
  fully retired year.
- Include wages whenever either partner is still working.
- Reconcile displayed sources to uses, including taxes and Medicare surcharges.
- Add a visible "taxes" segment if withdrawals include money used to pay taxes.
- Add an invariant test asserting that displayed sources and uses reconcile within a
  small rounding tolerance.


8. THE PROJECTION CHARTS OVERSTATE THEIR STATISTICAL MEANING
------------------------------------------------------------

Severity: Medium; mostly a communication and decision-quality issue.

The bold balance line is one simulated path selected because its final balance is near
the median. It is not the median balance at every age. The surrounding band uses the
absolute minimum and maximum among all paths, not a conventional confidence or
percentile interval. Extreme Gaussian outcomes can dominate the chart scale.

Recommendation:

- Calculate per-year percentiles such as P10, P25, P50, P75, and P90.
- Draw P50 as the median line and P10-P90 or P25-P75 as labeled bands.
- Avoid calling a single median-ending path "most likely."
- State the percentile definition directly below the chart.


9. THE SUCCESS LABEL DOES NOT DISCLOSE THE LEGACY-GOAL TEST
-----------------------------------------------------------

Severity: Medium when a legacy goal is entered.

The displayed metric says "Chance of never running out," but a path is counted as a
success only when it remains solvent and finishes above the legacy goal. The score can
therefore fall even though the portfolio never runs out.

Recommendation:

- When legacyGoal is zero, use "Chance of never running out."
- When it is positive, use language such as "Chance of funding retirement and leaving
  at least $X."
- Consider displaying solvency probability and legacy-goal probability separately.


10. IMPORTANT MODEL LIMITATIONS SHOULD BE DISCLOSED
---------------------------------------------------

Severity: Product-scope dependent.

The engine uses fixed inflation, normally distributed annual returns, a fixed
stock/bond correlation, and a fixed household filing status. It does not appear to model
mortality, survivor transitions, long-term care events, investment fees, or detailed
state-specific tax rules. These can matter more than small tax-bracket differences.

Recommendation:

- Publish a concise methodology and limitations page.
- Avoid presenting a whole-number score as actuarial certainty.
- Add sensitivity or stress scenarios for longevity, inflation, fees, and healthcare.
- Consider mortality/survivor modeling if the product is intended for planning beyond
  simple deterministic household horizons.


PART 2: LATENCY FINDINGS
========================

MEASURED BEHAVIOR
-----------------

On the reviewed machine:

- Opening Rework took approximately 960 milliseconds.
- A warm full 5,000-path compute took approximately 263-384 milliseconds.
- A 10,000-path compute took approximately 495-581 milliseconds.
- A 5,000-path score-only quickSuccess took approximately 73-123 milliseconds.
- Suggested adjustments and slider interactions completed in approximately 340
  milliseconds to one second, depending on warm-up and rendering.


ROOT CAUSE
----------

At 5,000 configured paths, a full compute performs approximately:

- 5,000 paths for the headline result.
- 7,497 paths across nine sustainable-spending bisection rounds.
- 2,668 paths for the base and three suggested moves.
- Total: approximately 15,165 lifetime path simulations per change.

It also:

- Builds yearly logs even for calculations that need only final solvency.
- Sorts full results.
- Materializes roughly 190,000 chart point objects.
- Calculates sustainable spending and suggested moves on screens that do not use them.
- Performs this work synchronously on the browser's main thread.

Opening Rework calculates both the filed and proposed plans, roughly doubling this work.
Publishing also causes both values to be recalculated. Returning to another screen
recomputes unchanged results because there is no shared application-level cache.


RECOMMENDED PERFORMANCE ARCHITECTURE
====================================

1. SPLIT THE ENGINE INTO PURPOSE-SPECIFIC MODES
-----------------------------------------------

Create separate conceptual APIs:

- computeScore(params, options): success count/rate and verdict only.
- computeSummary(params, options): score plus median summary and paycheck.
- computeReport(params, options): full logs, charts, sustainable spending, and levers.

Rework should use computeScore. Projection screens should request computeReport only
when opened. The questionnaire can use a fast score preview and request a full report
after the user pauses or leaves the page.


2. MAKE PATH LOGGING OPTIONAL
-----------------------------

The score, sustainable-spending search, and lever tests need final solvency and final
balance, not a yearly log for every path.

Recommended shape:

- simulateOutcome(..., captureLog=false) for score-only work.
- Capture logs only for the authoritative report paths used by charts.

This should reduce allocations, garbage collection, and computation time substantially.


3. USE A TWO-STAGE LIVE RESULT
------------------------------

During a drag or rapid series of changes:

- Calculate a 500-1,000-path preview.
- Debounce requests by approximately 100-150 milliseconds.
- Mark the value as "estimating" if necessary.

After pointer release, keyboard pause, idle time, or Publish:

- Calculate the authoritative 5,000-path result.
- Replace the preview when complete.

Because the engine already uses deterministic path seeds, previews can use the first N
paths of the same sequence and refine predictably.


4. MOVE MONTE CARLO WORK OFF THE MAIN THREAD
---------------------------------------------

Run simulation work in a Web Worker so controls, animations, and text updates remain
responsive. Assign each request an incrementing ID and ignore stale responses when a
newer input arrives.

Debouncing alone reduces the number of freezes but does not eliminate each freeze.
React transitions also do not move CPU-heavy JavaScript off the main thread.


5. CACHE DETERMINISTIC RESULTS
------------------------------

The fixed path seeds make memoization safe.

- Normalize validated parameters into a stable cache key.
- Include calculation mode and path count in the key.
- Store the filed-plan result at the app-shell level.
- Reuse it across Cover, Rework, Projection, and Income & Odds.
- Cache common suggested scenarios and share them across screens.
- Apply a bounded least-recently-used policy so the cache cannot grow indefinitely.

This will make Rework open immediately when the filed plan has already been calculated
and will eliminate cross-screen score drift.


6. REMOVE DUPLICATE CALCULATIONS
--------------------------------

- Reuse the filed headline sample when calculating lever baselines.
- Do not calculate sustainable spending on Rework.
- Do not rebuild full chart arrays until a chart screen needs them.
- Do not calculate the same base success score again inside Income & Odds.
- Consider pre-generating deterministic market shocks and reusing them across scenarios.


7. SHIP A PRODUCTION BUILD
--------------------------

The app currently loads React development builds and compiles JSX in the browser with
Babel. This is not the main Monte Carlo bottleneck, but it increases startup work,
render overhead, dependency risk, and production complexity.

Recommendation:

- Precompile JSX.
- Use production React.
- Bundle and minify application code.
- Keep dependency versions locked.
- Extract the pure calculation engine from the legacy DOM-oriented engine file.


PART 3: RELIABILITY, ACCESSIBILITY, AND MAINTAINABILITY
======================================================

1. There is no automated test suite, build configuration, type checking, or continuous
   integration visible in the project.

2. The current app loads a large legacy engine after DOMContentLoaded specifically so
   the engine's legacy DOM listeners do not run. This works but is brittle. A pure engine
   module would be easier to test, run in a worker, and reuse safely.

3. index.html and cover.html are currently identical. Maintaining two entry files
   creates unnecessary drift risk.

4. Navigation uses clickable spans rather than semantic links or buttons. Sliders do
   not have field-specific accessible names, and repeated plus/minus buttons are
   ambiguous to screen readers.

5. Much of the small secondary text uses approximately 3.29:1 contrast, below the usual
   4.5:1 target for small text.

6. The mobile design is visually strong but exposes only Cover and Questionnaire.
   Rework, Projection, and Income & Odds are unavailable on mobile.

7. Engine-load failure resolves into placeholder results rather than a clear blocking
   error, so a failed dependency could look like an extremely poor retirement plan.


PART 4: RECOMMENDED IMPLEMENTATION ORDER
========================================

PHASE 1: PROTECT ACCURACY
-------------------------

1. Add centralized schema validation and cross-field validation.
2. Add golden tests for known household projections.
3. Correct cohort-specific RMD and Social Security ages.
4. Correct couple IRMAA and its two-year lookback.
5. Introduce versioned 2026 statutory assumptions.
6. Unify scenario definitions across every screen.
7. Correct paycheck and income-source reconciliation.
8. Replace chart extrema and median-ending-path displays with per-year percentiles.


PHASE 2: REMOVE INTERACTION LATENCY
-----------------------------------

1. Add score-only, summary, and report calculation modes.
2. Disable path logs for score-only work.
3. Cache the filed-plan result and scenario results.
4. Add 500-1,000-path debounced previews.
5. Move calculations into a Web Worker.
6. Run the full 5,000-path calculation after idle/release/publish.


PHASE 3: PRODUCTION HARDENING
-----------------------------

1. Extract the pure engine from legacy DOM code.
2. Add a production build and remove browser-side Babel.
3. Add CI for tests, formatting, and static checks.
4. Improve accessibility semantics, labels, keyboard support, and contrast.
5. Add mobile access to all major screens or explain intentional mobile limitations.
6. Add methodology, limitations, and assumptions-update disclosures.


PART 5: MINIMUM VALIDATION SUITE
===============================

Before treating scores as production-grade, add at least these tests:

- Determinism: identical inputs produce identical results.
- Monotonicity: increasing spending cannot improve success when other inputs are fixed.
- Monotonicity: adding assets cannot reduce success when other inputs are fixed.
- Cross-screen consistency: the same scenario produces the same score everywhere.
- Couple reconciliation: income sources, taxes, and spending reconcile by year.
- RMD cohort tests for ages 73 and 75.
- Social Security FRA tests by birth year.
- IRMAA tests below, at, and above each threshold for one and two partners.
- Contribution-limit tests for 2025, 2026, and projected future years.
- Invalid-input and malicious-plan-file tests.
- Legacy-goal tests separating solvency from goal achievement.
- Regression fixtures checked against an independent spreadsheet or trusted planning
  calculation for a small set of representative households.


WHAT SHOULD BE PRESERVED
========================

- The magazine-like visual identity and strong information hierarchy.
- The Rework draft/publish mental model.
- Deterministic common random paths for fair scenario comparisons.
- Plain-language input explanations and progressive advanced settings.
- A single shared parameter state across screens.
- Local-only plan storage and portable JSON saves, after validation is strengthened.


BOTTOM LINE
===========

The best next move is not to tune the existing full compute loop. First separate quick
exploration from full-report calculation. In parallel, correct the cohort, IRMAA,
current-law, validation, and cross-screen consistency issues and lock them down with
golden tests.

After those changes, the application can preserve its current experience while becoming
both substantially faster and much easier to trust.
