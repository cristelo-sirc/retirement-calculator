What are Spending Guardrails?

Guardrails are a dynamic spending strategy that adjusts your withdrawals based on portfolio performance. Instead of withdrawing a fixed amount regardless of market conditions, guardrails help you adapt.

Each year, your withdrawal rate is recalculated:

Withdrawal Rate = Annual Spending ÷ Portfolio Balance

If markets drop and your rate gets too high (ceiling), spending is cut to preserve the portfolio. If markets soar and your rate gets too low (floor), spending can increase. This helps avoid both running out of money AND unnecessarily underspending.

Example

You retire with $2M and spend $80k/year (4% rate). Markets crash, and your portfolio drops to $1.5M. Your withdrawal rate is now 5.3% ($80k ÷ $1.5M).

If your ceiling is 6%, no action is needed yet. But if the portfolio drops further to $1.2M, your rate hits 6.7% — exceeding the ceiling — so spending is automatically cut by 10% to $72k.

What is TCJA Sunset?

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) of 2017 temporarily lowered federal income tax brackets through 2025. Unless Congress extends these provisions, tax rates will automatically revert to higher pre-2017 levels starting in 2026.

Examples of Bracket Changes:

12% bracket → 15% (+25% increase in rate)

22% bracket → 25% (+14% increase in rate)

24% bracket → 28% (+17% increase in rate)

Impact on Your Plan

If enabled, this calculator models higher taxes starting in 2026, which increases your projected tax liability and reduces sustainable spending in retirement. This helps you plan conservatively for potential tax increases.

What is an Equity Glide Path?

A glide path is a strategy where you gradually shift from stocks to bonds as you age, reducing portfolio volatility over time.

Example Glide Path

Age 60: 70% stocks, 30% bonds

Age 70: 60% stocks, 40% bonds

Age 80: 50% stocks, 50% bonds

Why Use a Glide Path?

As you age, you have less time to recover from market downturns. Gradually reducing stock exposure helps protect your portfolio from late-life market crashes while still maintaining growth potential in early retirement.

Settings: Specify your starting equity %, ending equity %, and the age range over which the transition occurs.

How Do Spousal Social Security Benefits Work?

You may qualify for a spousal benefit equal to 50% of your spouse's Social Security benefit at their Full Retirement Age (FRA).

Key Rules:

✓ Both spouses must have filed for their own benefits before spousal benefits begin

✓ You receive the higher of: your own benefit OR 50% of spouse's benefit (at your FRA)

✓ Claiming before your FRA reduces the spousal benefit

Example

Your benefit: $1,200/month

Spouse's benefit: $3,000/month

Spousal benefit (at your FRA): $1,500/month (50% of $3,000)

You receive: $1,500/month (the higher amount)

Note: This calculator automatically applies these rules when both of you have Social Security benefits entered.
